The AIEL Monitor

A public monitor of how artificial intelligence is moving through the Swedish labour market: the skills employers demand, how exposed each occupation is, how far adoption and everyday use have spread, whether AI augments or replaces tasks, and what that means for entry-level opportunity and job quality. Built on public data so it can be cited and refreshed automatically, and committed to no funder. Three views are live today; the rest are on an open roadmap.

AI in Demand · coming next

Who is the AI for? ◔ Preview · not yet measured

The next release splits AI-mentioning ads by what the worker does with AI. Our current lexical measure already hints at the split; the semantic layer, now training, will classify each ad properly.

Builders

Ads asking someone to create AI: ML engineers, data scientists, research roles requiring specific techniques.

Integrators

Ads asking someone to wire AI in: software, MLOps and product roles that use AI components.

Users

Ads where AI is simply a tool of the job: marketing, admin, teaching, sales. The largest, fastest-growing tier.

Module · live · across countries

How AI-exposed is each country's workforce?

Because DAIOE scores occupations (ISCO-08), not just Swedish jobs, we can place Sweden in international context on public data. Each country's score is the employment-weighted average DAIOE generative-AI exposure (v2023) across its occupational mix, weighted by Eurostat EU-LFS employment in 2023 (a few countries use their latest year, marked ’YY). Exposure is not displacement: in our cross-country panel, exposure predicts occupational growth as often as decline. It shows where AI overlaps with the work, no more.

How to read this

What we measure, and what we don't yet.

The AI in Demand module reads every open and historical advertisement in Sweden's public job board (Platsbanken / JobTech), 2006–2025: about 10.9 million ads. An ad counts as AI-in-demand when its text requests an AI skill, matched by a versioned, citable term list (Swedish and English). A semantic layer, now training, will catch AI ads that use no listed term.

Caveats, in plain sight

  • 2025 is provisional: the current-year bulk file is still filling; the latest point may revise up.
  • The lexical measure is a lower bound; roughly three times as many ads mention AI in passing without naming a technique.
  • The occupation snapshot is a live sample of open ads, not the full year.
  • The builder / integrator / user split is a preview and not yet a measured series.

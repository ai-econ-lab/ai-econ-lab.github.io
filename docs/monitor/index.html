The AIEL Monitor

How artificial intelligence is reshaping work: measured internationally through a portable exposure measure (DAIOE) and public data, and in unusual depth for Sweden via job ads, firm surveys and administrative registers. One spine of four questions: how exposed the work is, how much employers demand AI, how far firms have adopted it, and what it means for jobs. Built on public data so every figure can be cited and refreshed automatically, and committed to no funder.

Module 1 · Exposure · across countries

How AI-exposed is each country's workforce?

DAIOE scores occupations (ISCO-08), not only Swedish jobs, so every country sits on one public-data scale. Each score is the employment-weighted average DAIOE generative-AI exposure (v2023) across a country's occupational mix, weighted by Eurostat EU-LFS employment in 2023 (a few countries use their latest year, marked ’YY). Exposure is not displacement: in our panel it predicts occupational growth as often as decline, showing only where AI overlaps with the work.

Sweden, in depth

Sweden's workforce is the 2nd most exposed of 36 countries (employment coverage ≈100%). The occupation-by-occupation detail behind the measure lives on the DAIOE page, and Swedish employment is set against exposure over time in the Occupations Explorer below. Register data give the depth this public-data view cannot; the two are complements.

Coming next · who is the AI for? ◔ Preview · not yet measured

The next release splits AI-mentioning ads by what the worker does with AI. Our current lexical measure already hints at the split; the semantic layer, now training, will classify each ad properly.

Builders

Ads asking someone to create AI: ML engineers, data scientists, research roles requiring specific techniques.

Integrators

Ads asking someone to wire AI in: software, MLOps and product roles that use AI components.

Users

Ads where AI is simply a tool of the job: marketing, admin, teaching, sales. The largest, fastest-growing tier.

Module 3 · Adoption · across countries

How widely have firms actually adopted AI?

Exposure is potential; adoption is what firms have done. The share of enterprises using at least one AI technology (2025, Eurostat, isoc_eb_ai (E_AI_TANY)), with the year-on-year change since 2024 shown as +pp. Adoption is climbing fast: the EU average rose from 8% in 2023 to 19.9% in 2025. Exposure and adoption need not line up across countries.

↓ Data (CSV)↓ SVGSource: Eurostat, isoc_eb_ai (E_AI_TANY), 2025 (change vs 2024) · % of enterprises (10+ employed)

Sweden, in depth · by firm size

Sweden is among the EU leaders at 35% in 2025, up from 25.1% a year earlier (Eurostat). SCB's firm survey decomposes that headline by size: adoption climbs steeply with the size of the firm, from 31% of small firms (10–49 employees) to 72% of large ones (250+), every class up sharply since 2021. The all-firms figure (35%, highlighted) is the same number the cross-country bar shows.

What does it mean for jobs and job quality?

Exposure and demand are inputs; outcomes are what happens to workers. Three views: employment by occupation, working conditions, and the entry-level "canaries" signal on vacancies.

Working conditions and AI exposure

More AI-exposed occupations are the classic "active job": more mentally demanding, but with more control over one's work; harder to switch off after hours, yet more meaningful and markedly more positive about technology. Public survey data by occupation, set against DAIOE generative-AI (v2023); descriptive, not causal.

Toggle gender: the control gap is the story. In low-exposure jobs women report far less influence than men (56% vs 68%); in high-exposure jobs it nearly closes (74% vs 78%).

Entry-level squeeze

In the most AI-exposed occupations, a smaller share of openings ask for no prior experience than in the least-exposed occupations, every year since 2020, and the gap has widened from −3.1pp to −5.3pp in 2025. An independent, ad-based echo of the Canaries finding; descriptive, not causal (less-exposed work skews lower-skill, so part of the level gap is structural, the widening is the signal). The same entry-level pattern appears in the international AI "canaries" literature on young workers, though no directly comparable cross-country series exists yet.

How to read this

What we measure, and what we don't yet.

The measure runs on public data throughout. The Swedish demand series reads every open and historical advertisement in Sweden's public job board (Platsbanken / JobTech), 2006–2025: about 10.9 million ads. An ad counts as AI-in-demand when its text requests an AI skill, matched by a versioned, citable term list (Swedish and English); a semantic layer, now training, will catch AI ads that use no listed term. Exposure, adoption and cross-country demand come from DAIOE (generative-AI, v2023), Eurostat and the Stanford AI Index.

Caveats, in plain sight

  • 2025 is provisional: the current-year bulk file is still filling; the latest point may revise up.
  • The lexical measure is a lower bound; roughly three times as many ads mention AI in passing without naming a technique.
  • The occupation snapshot is a live sample of open ads, not the full year.
  • The builder / integrator / user split is a preview and not yet a measured series.

The AIEL Monitor

How artificial intelligence is reshaping work: measured internationally through a portable exposure measure (DAIOE) and public data, and in unusual depth for Sweden via job ads, firm surveys and administrative registers. One spine of four questions: how exposed the work is, how much employers demand AI, how far firms have adopted it, and what it means for jobs. Built on public data so every figure can be cited and refreshed automatically, and committed to no funder.

Module 1 · Exposure · across countries

How much of each country's work is AI-exposed?

DAIOE scores every occupation (ISCO-08) for how far generative AI overlaps with its tasks. We label the top 25% of occupations by that score the most AI-exposed; the bars show the share of each country's jobs in them (Eurostat EU-LFS employment, 2023; a few countries use their latest year, marked ’YY). So 39% means about four in ten Swedish jobs sit in the most AI-exposed quarter of occupations. Exposure is not displacement: in our panel it predicts occupational growth as often as decline, showing only where AI overlaps with the work.

Sweden, in depth · our live measure

How often Swedish employers ask for AI skills in their job ads, by year, by occupation, and soon by whether they want AI built, integrated, or simply used. We name the series by what the employer does, never by what the job is: ads that ask for AI skills, ads that name an AI skill. We deliberately avoid the phrase "AI jobs". We read every open and historical Swedish job ad (JobTech / Platsbanken, 2006 onwards, about 10.9 million) with a versioned, citable term list, so the level and its fifteen-fold rise since 2006 are reproducible.

Coming next · who is the AI for? ◔ Preview · not yet measured

The next release splits AI-mentioning ads by what the worker does with AI. Our current lexical measure already hints at the split; the semantic layer, now training, will classify each ad properly.

Builders

Ads asking someone to create AI: ML engineers, data scientists, research roles requiring specific techniques.

Integrators

Ads asking someone to wire AI in: software, MLOps and product roles that use AI components.

Users

Ads where AI is simply a tool of the job: marketing, admin, teaching, sales. The largest, fastest-growing tier.

Module 3 · Adoption · across countries

How widely have firms actually adopted AI?

Exposure is potential; adoption is what firms have done. The share of enterprises using at least one AI technology (2025, Eurostat, isoc_eb_ai (E_AI_TANY)), with the year-on-year change since 2024 shown as +pp. Adoption is climbing fast: the EU average rose from 8% in 2023 to 19.9% in 2025. Exposure and adoption need not line up across countries.

Sweden, in depth · by firm size

Sweden is among the EU leaders at 35% in 2025, up from 25.1% a year earlier (Eurostat). SCB's firm survey decomposes that headline by size: adoption climbs steeply with the size of the firm, from 31% of small firms (10–49 employees) to 72% of large ones (250+), every class up sharply since 2021. The all-firms figure (35%, highlighted) is the same number the cross-country bar shows.

Sweden, in depth · by worker

Firm surveys count employers who have started. This counts people: the share of professionals who use AI in their own work rose from 32% in May 2023 to 77% in May 2025, and daily use went from 3% to 22% over the same period, so the shift is in intensity as much as reach. Nearly everyone now works somewhere AI is used at all (92% in 2024, 97% in 2025). The spread by profession is wide and narrowing: communicators are near saturation while lawyers remain furthest behind, and central government trails the private sector by 30pp. Men 81%, women 75%.

Data shared with the lab by Akavia. Akavia surveys its members through a web panel covering economists, lawyers, IT academics, social scientists, HR specialists and communicators. Between about 3,000 and 3,800 members answer each round, with response rates from a quarter to just under a half. Figures describe Akavia's members in these professions, not the Swedish labour market as a whole, and should be read with care because the sample is not an unrestricted random sample of the population.

Working conditions and AI exposure

More AI-exposed occupations are the classic "active job": more mentally demanding, but with more control over one's work; harder to switch off after hours, yet more meaningful and markedly more positive about technology. Public survey data by occupation, set against DAIOE generative-AI (v2023); descriptive, not causal.

Use, governance and who pays

Among Swedish professional-union members, workplace governance runs well behind actual use. In 2025, 77% used AI at work while only 50% knew of a policy and 38% of a strategy, a gap of 27pp. The figures say knows of rather than has: about a fifth answer that they do not know, which is counted here as not knowing of one. Training runs the same way: 90% want to develop their AI skills, 33% have been offered it by an employer, against 82% and 6% in 2023.

And who provides the tools. Among those using standalone AI tools, not among all workers, 50% have a private e-mail account connected to a work AI tool, the employer pays for 33% and 15% pay themselves. The denominator matters here and is easy to overstate.

Entry-level squeeze

In the most AI-exposed occupations, a smaller share of openings ask for no prior experience than in the least-exposed occupations, every year since 2020, and the gap has widened from −3.1pp to −5.3pp in 2025. An independent, ad-based echo of the Canaries finding; descriptive, not causal (less-exposed work skews lower-skill, so part of the level gap is structural, the widening is the signal). The same entry-level pattern appears in the international AI "canaries" literature on young workers, though no directly comparable cross-country series exists yet.

How to read this

What we measure, and what we don't yet.

The measure runs on public data with one named exception. The Swedish demand series reads every open and historical advertisement in Sweden's public job board (Platsbanken / JobTech), 2006 onwards: about 10.9 million ads. An ad counts when its text names an AI skill; the stricter floor counts it only when the skill sits in the role's own tasks or requirements. The term list is versioned and citable (Swedish and English; frozen v1, fingerprint 2f073672be5d998c), and a semantic layer, now training, will catch AI ads that use no listed term. Exposure, adoption and cross-country demand come from DAIOE (generative-AI, v2023), Eurostat and the Stanford AI Index.

The exception is the worker-side layer, which comes from Akavia, a Swedish professional union that surveys its members through a web panel and shares the de-identified results with the lab. We publish aggregated figures with attribution and keep the underlying records private; cells below 50 respondents are never shown. Akavia does not fund the lab and does not see results before publication. The processing, and any error in it, is ours.

Caveats, in plain sight

  • 2026 is partial: the first half comes from the Q1–Q2 archives and the latest weeks from the live feed; the newest point will revise.
  • The chart ends with the June archives; the live feed runs to today and joins the chart once the two sources are reconciled.
  • The 2022–2023 dip coincides with the global tech-hiring downturn and a sharp expansion of total ad volume: read it as a market swing, not necessarily as falling AI intent per employer.
  • The headline lines are deliberately strict: roughly 2.5 times as many ads mention AI in some form. That broad bound mixes genuine demand with company boilerplate in proportions that drift over time, so we publish it only after an era-specific correction, now in progress.
  • Ads that mention AI only when describing the company never count; an ad counts for what it asks of the person being hired.
  • The builder / integrator / user split is a preview and not yet a measured series.
